# Step 0: Key Terminology

In socio-ecological research, ensuring precision in terminology is crucial for designing rigorous studies, measuring constructs accurately, and bridging the gap between social sciences and conservation practice. Below is a glossary of core terms often used in psychometrics, psychology, and qualitative research within the socio-ecological context.

*(Note: The alternative names for inductive and deductive coding have been aligned with standard qualitative methodology definitions, as they are sometimes interchanged in casual use).*

## Box: Glossary of Socio-Ecological Research Terms

**Latent construct**  
Unobservable cognitive or social variables, such as wildlife tolerance, that cannot be measured directly but are inferred from multiple observable indicators or items (Browne-Nuñez et al., 2015; Vaske, 2008). 

**Observed construct (or Observed Variable)**  
A characteristic or variable that can be directly measured, quantified, or recorded by a researcher. In psychometrics, observed variables serve as the foundational indicators (e.g., specific survey questions) used to estimate a latent construct (DeVellis, 2016).

**Psychometric scale**  
A standardized measurement instrument composed of multiple items (questions or statements) designed to capture the true variance in a latent construct while minimizing measurement error. This allows researchers to quantitatively measure complex psychological traits (Nunnally & Bernstein, 1994; DeVellis, 2016).

**Inductive coding**  
An approach to qualitative data analysis where codes and themes emerge directly from the data itself, rather than from preconceived theories. Also referred to as bottom-up, emergent, open, or reflexive coding in various grounded theory approaches (Charmaz, 2006; Saldaña, 2021).

**Deductive coding**  
A qualitative approach where researchers apply pre-existing theoretical frameworks, hypotheses, or codebooks to evaluate the data. Also referred to as top-down, a priori, confirmatory, or selective coding (Crabtree & Miller, 1999; Saldaña, 2021).

**Informed consent**  
An ethical and legal necessity in research involving human subjects. It ensures that participants are fully aware of the study's purpose, the extent of their involvement, potential risks and benefits, and their right to withdraw at any time prior to their participation (American Psychological Association, 2017).

**Likert-scale**  
A commonly used psychometric response scale in which respondents specify their level of agreement or disagreement to a series of statements. It typically relies on a symmetric scale (e.g., ranging from "strongly disagree" to "strongly agree") formatted to measure intensity of feelings (Likert, 1932; Vaske, 2008).

**Hierarchical axial coding**  
An advanced phase in qualitative data analysis that relates codes to each other. It involves grouping initial open codes into broader categories or hierarchies to identify relationships, interactions, and overarching themes within the data (Corbin & Strauss, 2014).

**Attitudes**  
A psychological tendency expressed by evaluating a particular entity (e.g., a species, a management action, or an ecosystem) with some degree of favor or disfavor. Attitudes are typically learned and can influence behavior (Eagly & Chaiken, 1993; Manfredo, 2008).

**Values**  
Deeply held, enduring beliefs about what is good or bad, right or wrong. Values transcend specific situations, change very slowly across a person's lifetime, and serve as guiding principles for life choices (Schwartz, 1992; Manfredo et al., 2014).

**Value orientation**  
The pattern of direction and intensity among a set of basic values that reflects how an individual adapts their fundamental values to a particular context, such as wildlife or forests (e.g., mutualism/biocentric vs. domination/anthropocentric orientations) (Fulton et al., 1996; Manfredo, 2008).

**Behavior**  
The observable, tangible actions taken by individuals or groups. In socio-ecological research, this often pertains to conservation actions, natural resource consumption, or policy compliance (Fishbein & Ajzen, 2010).

**Behavioral intentions**  
The cognitive representation of a person's readiness or willingness to perform a given behavior. It is often considered the most immediate antecedent and strongest predictor of actual behavior in theories like the Theory of Planned Behavior (Ajzen, 1991).

**Perception**  
The process by which individuals interpret, organize, and make sense of sensory information to produce a meaningful experience of their environment. Perceptions are heavily influenced by cognitive biases, culture, and personal context (Bennett et al., 2017).

**Motivation**  
The internal psychological drivers and processes that initiate, direct, and maintain goal-oriented behaviors. In a socio-ecological context, motivations explain *why* people engage in certain activities, such as outdoor recreation or environmental stewardship (Deci & Ryan, 2000; Manfredo, 2008).

**Social norms**  
The perceived standards, rules, or expectations of acceptable behavior within a given group or society. Norms often pressure individuals to conform to collective behaviors (Cialdini et al., 1990; Heberlein, 2012).

**Injunctive norms**  
A specific subset of social norms that reflects an individual's perception of what behaviors are approved or disapproved of by others. It is the moral assessment of what one *ought* to do (Cialdini et al., 1990).

**Affect**  
A broad range of feelings that individuals experience, encompassing both specific emotions and general moods. Affect often acts as a rapid, pre-cognitive, and intuitive response to an environmental stimulus or wildlife encounter (Slovic et al., 2007; Jacobs et al., 2012).

**Risk**  
The subjective assessment or cognitive perception of the probability and severity of a negative outcome. In socio-ecological research, this frequently relates to the perceived risk of human-wildlife conflict or environmental hazards (Sjöberg, 2000; Dickman, 2010).
