# Theory Summary

This document summarizes specific theories, key frameworks, and theory syntheses regarding human action and transformative change drawn from the collected literature. 

**Note to Ecologists & Conservation Researchers:** 
Just as ecological investigations are grounded in underlying drivers (e.g., Theory of Island Biogeography to explain extinction/speciation), survey design requires a theoretical framework to explain *why* people act the way they do. For an excellent, comprehensive overview of how to select and apply different frameworks specifically for conservation contexts, we highly recommend **Stern (2018)** *Social science theory for environmental sustainability: a practical guide*.

The table below outlines various theories, their main arguments, constructs, contexts of benefit, and limitations.

| Theory / Framework | Main Argument | Main Constructs | Contextual Benefits & Applicability | Limitations | Source |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Behavioral Decision Theory** | Individuals make decisions based on cognitive processes that deviate from perfect rationality, relying on heuristics. | Expected utility, heuristics, cognitive biases, value. | Useful for understanding choices under uncertainty or risk, e.g., adopting new farming practices. | Focuses heavily on cognitive limitations; may underplay structural or emotional drivers. | Edwards (1954); Tversky & Kahneman (1974) |
| **Behavioral Intention Theories (TRA, TPB, TAM)** | Behavioral intention is the primary driver of behavior and is influenced by attitudes, subjective norms, usefulness, and perceived control. | Attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioral control, perceived ease of use. | Excellent for predicting specific, volitional behaviors where individual choice is primary. | Less predictive for habitual behaviors, emotional actions, or heavily structurally constrained acts. | Fishbein & Ajzen (1975); Davis (1989); Ajzen (1991) |
| **Cognitive Load Theory** | Learning and task execution are constrained by working memory capacity; systems should be designed to optimize this load. | Intrinsic cognitive load, extraneous cognitive load, germane cognitive load, working memory. | Important for designing effective educational programs and materials, including public outreach. | Primarily focused on individual learning processes, not on broader social or structural factors. | Sweller (1988); Sweller et al. (1998) |
| **Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM)** | Persuasion operates through two routes (central and peripheral) depending on the user's motivation and ability to process information. | Central route processing, peripheral route processing, motivation, ability to process. | Useful in designing communication strategies that aim to change attitudes or behaviors. | Assumes a level of rationality and motivation in the audience that may not always be present. | Petty & Cacioppo (1986) |
| **Flow Theory** | Optimal human experience occurs when there is a perfect balance between personal skill and task challenge, causing deep immersion. | Skill level, challenge magnitude, immersion/absorption, goal clarity, immediate feedback. | Applicable in educational, work, and recreational settings to enhance engagement and satisfaction. | May be difficult to achieve in practice; not all tasks have a clear challenge-skills balance. | Csikszentmihalyi (1975, 1990) |
| **Homo Ludens (Play Theory)** | Play is a primary, voluntary element of human culture that exists in a "magic circle" separate from ordinary life. | Magic circle, voluntary participation, rules, meaning creation, playfulness. | Highlights the importance of play and recreation in human experience; useful in designing engaging interventions. | May be seen as trivializing serious issues; not all contexts value play equally. | Huizinga (1938) |
| **Information Processing Theory** | The human mind operates similarly to a computer, processing information through sensory, working, and long-term memory. | Sensory memory, working memory, long-term memory, encoding, retrieval. | Informs the design of educational and informational materials by optimizing how information is presented. | Oversimplifies human cognition; doesn't account for emotional or social influences on learning. | Miller (1956); Schunk (1996) |
| **Keller’s ARCS Motivational Model** | Instructional (and system) design must explicitly account for the motivation to learn by securing attention and building relevance. | Attention, Relevance, Confidence, Satisfaction (ARCS). | Provides a clear framework for designing motivating and engaging educational experiences. | Primarily focused on individual learning; may not address broader systemic or contextual factors. | Keller (1987) |
| **Organizational Learning Theory** | Organizations learn and adapt over time by processing information from their environment and modifying internal behaviors/structures. | Knowledge acquisition, information distribution, interpretation, organizational memory. | Useful for understanding and improving organizational change and development processes. | Can be abstract and difficult to apply in practice; may overlook power dynamics within organizations. | Argyris & Schön (1978); Daft & Weick (1984) |
| **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** | Human motivation is driven by intrinsic psychological needs, and environments that support these needs foster the highest engagement. | Autonomy, competence, relatedness, intrinsic motivation, extrinsic motivation. | Highlights the importance of intrinsic motivation in learning and behavior change; widely applicable. | May be challenging to implement in systems or contexts that do not value or understand intrinsic motivation. | Deci & Ryan (1985, 2000) |
| **Social Cognitive Theory / Social Learning** | People learn by observing others within the context of social interactions, experiences, and outside media, mediated by their own beliefs. | Observational learning, self-efficacy, reciprocal determinism, behavioral reinforcement. | Emphasizes the role of social influence and modeling in behavior change; applicable in many contexts. | Can be difficult to measure or quantify; may underestimate individual cognitive processes. | Bandura (1977, 1986, 2001) |
| **8 Metatheories of Human Action** | Human action theories across disciplines can be organized into eight fundamental assumptions (metatheories), such as "independent," "needs," "top-down," and "interdependent" metatheories. No single metatheory addresses all problems; integration is needed to tackle complex social-environmental crises. | Independent vs. Interdependent Variables, Needs/Motivations, Structural/Top-Down Constraints, Metatheory frameworks. | Provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the complexity of human behavior; encourages integrative approaches. | Can be overly complex or abstract; may be challenging to apply in practical research or intervention design. | Eyster et al. (2022) |
| **Narrative Transportation Theory** | Individuals become absorbed in a story context, connecting with characters and the narrative world while disconnecting from their physical environment, which results in reduced counterarguing and unique persuasive effects. | Narrative transportation, immersion, absorption, perspective-taking, counterarguing. | Highlights the power of storytelling in communication and persuasion; applicable in media, education, and therapy. | Not all individuals or cultures may respond to narratives in the same way; potential ethical concerns in manipulation. | Green & Brock (2000) |
| **Persuasion Knowledge Model** | Consumers develop knowledge about persuasion attempts, tactics, and agents, and they use this knowledge to cope with and respond to persuasive messages. | Persuasion knowledge, agent knowledge, topic knowledge, coping mechanisms. | Useful for understanding resistance to persuasion and designing more effective communication strategies. | Assumes a level of cognitive processing and awareness that may not be present in all individuals or situations. | Friestad & Wright (1994) |
| **Associative Network Theory** | Information is stored in memory as interconnected nodes; activating one node spreads activation to related nodes, facilitating recall and processing of linked concepts. | Memory nodes, associative links, spreading activation, recall. | Informs the design of educational and informational materials by optimizing how information is structured and connected. | Oversimplifies the complexity of human memory and learning; may not account for individual differences in cognition. | Collins & Loftus (1975) |
| **Cognitive Capacity Theory** | The human cognitive system has a limited pool of resources; performance and information processing depend on how these resources are allocated during tasks. | Cognitive capacity, resource allocation, processing limits. | Important for understanding and optimizing human performance in complex tasks or learning situations. | Focuses on individual cognitive processes; may overlook social or environmental factors that influence performance. | Isen & Daubman (1984) |
| **Entertainment Overcoming Resistance Model** | Narrative structures and character involvement in entertainment media reduce various forms of audience resistance to persuasive messaging (such as psychological reactance or counterarguing). | Transportation, parasocial interaction, identification, psychological reactance. | Highlights the potential of entertainment media to effectively communicate persuasive messages and engage audiences. | Not all individuals or cultures may respond to entertainment narratives in the same way; potential ethical concerns in manipulation. | Moyer-Gusé & Nabi (2010) |
| **Exemplification Theory** | The use of specific, emotionally engaging examples (exemplars) in media profoundly influences audience formation of risk perceptions and beliefs more strongly than base-rate statistical data. | Exemplars, base-rate information, availability heuristic, risk perception. | Useful for designing effective risk communication and public health messages. | May oversimplify complex issues; not all individuals may respond to exemplars in the same way. | Zillmann (1999) |
| **Identification Theory** | An imaginative process wherein the audience member assumes the identity, goals, and perspective of a character, essentially experiencing the narrative from the inside. | Character identification, empathy, perspective-taking, absorption. | Highlights the potential of narrative media to foster empathy and perspective-taking; applicable in media, education, and therapy. | Not all individuals or cultures may respond to narratives in the same way; potential ethical concerns in manipulation. | Cohen (2018) |
| **Psychological Reactance Theory** | When individuals feel their freedom of choice or action is threatened, they experience a reactionary motivational state directed toward restoring that freedom, often by rejecting a persuasive message. | Threat to freedom, psychological reactance, freedom restoration, boomerang effect. | Important for understanding resistance to persuasion and designing effective communication strategies. | Can be difficult to measure or quantify; may underestimate individual cognitive processes. | Brehm (1966) |
| **Social Identity Theory** | An individual's self-concept and self-esteem are significantly influenced by their meaningful memberships in various social groups, leading to in-group favoritism. | Social categorization, social identification, social comparison, in-group bias. | Helpful when studying conflict management, inter-group dynamics (e.g. fishers vs. regulators). | Can be abstract; difficult to isolate identity from other cultural and economic factors. | Hogg & Turner (1987) |
| **Taylor's Strategy Wheel** | A strategic framework in marketing communication that categorizes message strategies across transmission (informational, ego, social) and ritual (sensory, routine, acute) dimensions. | Transmission view, ritual view, informational/ego/social strategies. | Useful for designing effective marketing and communication strategies. | Primarily focused on marketing and communication contexts; may not be directly applicable to other fields. | Taylor (1999) |
| **Dual Processing Models** | Attitude change and persuasion occur via distinct processing routes (e.g., careful cognitive elaboration vs. superficial cues) depending on the individual's motivation and cognitive capacity. | Elaboration likelihood, central route, peripheral route, motivation, ability. | Informs the design of persuasive messages and interventions by tailoring to the audience's processing route. | Assumes a level of rationality and motivation in the audience that may not always be present. | Petty et al. (1983) |
| **Use of Human Action Theories in Conservation** | Most conservation research fails to leverage the full richness of human action theories. The field relies heavily on the *Theory of Planned Behavior* (driven by the "independent self" metatheory), limiting its capacity to foster transformative structural shifts in conservation. | Social Science Theory, Human Action, Theory of Planned Behavior, Independent Self Metatheory, Conservation Behavior. | Highlights the importance of integrating diverse human action theories in conservation research and practice. | Can be overly critical of existing approaches; may not provide clear solutions or alternatives. | Eyster et al. (2025) |
| **Gamification Theory Agenda** | Gamification in non-gaming systems lacks theoretical grounding; understanding its impacts requires integrating existing IS and psychological theories (like Self-Determination Theory, Flow, and Information Processing) into a cohesive research agenda for behavioral and attitudinal change. | Gamification, Attitudinal Change, Behavioral Change, Intrinsic/Extrinsic Motivation, Fun/Playfulness. | Provides a comprehensive framework for understanding and designing effective gamification interventions. | Can be overly focused on individual psychological processes; may overlook social or structural factors. | Treiblmaier et al. (2018) |
| **Integrative Framework for Transformative Social Change** | Achieving environmental sustainability requires overcoming the gap between reductionist (individual-focused) and holistic (structural) paradigms. Transformative change emerges from multi-directional interactions involving private, social-signaling, and system-changing actions. | Private Action, Social-Signaling Action, System-Changing Action, Social Structures, Reductionism vs. Holism. | Offers a comprehensive framework for understanding and facilitating transformative social change. | Can be overly complex or abstract; may be challenging to apply in practical research or intervention design. | Naito et al. (2022) |
| **Value-Belief-Norm (VBN) Theory** | Links ecological worldviews to awareness-of-consequences beliefs to ascription-of-responsibility beliefs to personal moral norms to pro-environmental action through a causal chain. | Biospheric values, ecological worldview (NEP), awareness of consequences, ascription of responsibility, personal norms. | Useful for understanding the psychological drivers of pro-environmental behavior; applicable in conservation and sustainability contexts. | Primarily focused on individual values and beliefs; may overlook structural or systemic factors. | Stern et al. (1999); Stern (2000) |
| **Norm Activation Model (NAM)** | Altruistic and pro-social behavior is activated when individuals become aware that their actions have consequences for others and ascribe responsibility for those consequences to themselves. | Awareness of consequences, ascription of responsibility, personal norms, altruistic behavior. | Highlights the importance of moral and ethical considerations in behavior change; applicable in many contexts. | Can be difficult to measure or quantify; may underestimate individual cognitive processes. | Schwartz (1977) |
| **New Ecological Paradigm (NEP)** | Measures the degree to which people hold an ecological (vs. anthropocentric) worldview, serving as a foundational predictor of environmental attitudes and behavior. | Ecological worldview, limits to growth, anti-anthropocentrism, balance of nature, human exemptionalism. | Useful for assessing and understanding individual and cultural differences in environmental attitudes and behaviors. | Primarily focused on worldview and values; may not directly translate to specific behavioral predictions. | Dunlap & Van Liere (1978); Dunlap et al. (2000) |
| **Nature Connectedness / Connectedness to Nature** | Individuals' emotional and cognitive sense of connection with the natural world is a stable predictor of pro-environmental attitudes and behavior beyond knowledge alone. | Nature relatedness, inclusion of nature in self, emotional affinity, cognitive connectedness. | Highlights the importance of fostering a deep, emotional connection with nature in promoting pro-environmental behavior. | May be challenging to measure or quantify; not all individuals or cultures may share the same conceptualization of nature. | Nisbet et al. (2009); Schultz (2002) |
| **Common-Pool Resource (CPR) Theory** | Under identifiable institutional conditions, communities can self-govern shared natural resources without privatization or top-down state regulation, contrary to the "tragedy of the commons." | Design principles, self-governance, institutional arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions. | Provides a framework for understanding and designing effective community-based resource management systems. | Primarily focused on institutional and structural factors; may overlook individual or cultural differences. | Ostrom (1990) |
| **Collective Action Theory** | Rational, self-interested individuals will tend to free-ride on group efforts for public goods unless selective incentives or institutional mechanisms are present. | Free-riding, public goods, group size, selective incentives, cooperation dilemma. | Useful for understanding and designing effective collective action and cooperation strategies. | Assumes a level of rationality and self-interest that may not always be present; can be overly simplistic. | Olson (1965) |
| **Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) Framework** | A general analytical framework for studying how rules-in-use, biophysical conditions, and community attributes structure human interactions and outcomes in social-ecological systems. | Action arenas, rules-in-use, biophysical conditions, community attributes, outcomes. | Provides a comprehensive framework for analyzing and understanding complex social-ecological systems. | Can be overly complex or abstract; may be challenging to apply in practical research or intervention design. | Ostrom (2005) |
| **Social-Ecological Systems (SES) Framework** | A diagnostic, multi-tier framework for identifying the key variables and interactions that shape outcomes in linked social and ecological systems, enabling cross-case comparison. | Resource systems, resource units, governance systems, actors/users, interactions, outcomes. | Useful for understanding and analyzing the complex interactions and dynamics in social-ecological systems. | Can be overly complex or abstract; may be challenging to apply in practical research or intervention design. | Ostrom (2009) |
| **Resilience Theory / Adaptive Cycle** | Social-ecological systems cycle through phases of growth, conservation, release, and reorganization; resilience is the capacity to absorb disturbance and reorganize while retaining essential function. | Adaptive cycle (r, K, Ω, α), resilience, adaptability, transformability. | Provides a framework for understanding and enhancing the resilience of social-ecological systems. | Can be overly focused on ecological systems; may not fully account for social or cultural factors. | Holling (1973); Folke et al. (2004) |
| **Panarchy Theory** | Adaptive cycles are nested across spatial and temporal scales in a hierarchy (panarchy), and cross-scale interactions (revolt and remember) explain how small, fast changes can cascade upward or how large, slow structures constrain lower levels. | Nested adaptive cycles, revolt, remember, cross-scale interactions. | Useful for understanding and analyzing the multi-scale dynamics and interactions in social-ecological systems. | Can be overly complex or abstract; may be challenging to apply in practical research or intervention design. | Gunderson & Holling (2002) |
| **Complex Adaptive Systems (CAS)** | Socio-ecological systems exhibit emergent properties, self-organization, and non-linear dynamics arising from interactions among diverse, adaptive agents, making prediction from component behavior alone insufficient. | Emergence, self-organization, non-linearity, feedback loops, agent diversity. | Provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the complexity and dynamics of socio-ecological systems. | Can be overly complex or abstract; may be challenging to apply in practical research or intervention design. | Holland (1995); Levin (1998) |
| **Transtheoretical Model / Stages of Change** | Behavior change proceeds through discrete motivational stages (precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance), and interventions are most effective when matched to the individual's current stage. | Stages of change, processes of change, decisional balance, self-efficacy. | Useful for designing and targeting effective behavior change interventions. | Can be overly simplistic; may not account for non-linear or cyclical patterns of behavior change. | Prochaska & DiClemente (1983) |
| **COM-B Model / Behavior Change Wheel** | Behavior is a product of the interaction between Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation; systematic intervention design requires diagnosing which of these components is deficient. | Capability (physical/psychological), Opportunity (physical/social), Motivation (reflective/automatic). | Provides a comprehensive framework for understanding and designing effective behavior change interventions. | Can be overly focused on individual psychological processes; may overlook social or structural factors. | Michie et al. (2011) |
| **Diffusion of Innovations** | Innovations spread through social systems via communication channels over time, with adoption following a predictable S-curve driven by adopter categories from innovators to laggards. | Innovation attributes (relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, observability), adopter categories, communication channels. | Useful for understanding and facilitating the adoption of new technologies or practices. | Primarily focused on the innovation and communication aspects; may not address broader social or structural factors. | Rogers (1962, 2003) |
| **Multi-Level Perspective (MLP) on Sustainability Transitions** | Sustainability transitions result from the dynamic interplay between innovative niches, prevailing sociotechnical regimes, and exogenous landscape pressures. | Niche innovations, sociotechnical regime, sociotechnical landscape, windows of opportunity. | Provides a comprehensive framework for understanding and facilitating sustainability transitions. | Can be overly complex or abstract; may be challenging to apply in practical research or intervention design. | Geels (2002) |
| **Transformative Learning Theory** | Perspective transformation occurs when disorienting dilemmas trigger critical reflection on taken-for-granted assumptions, leading to revised meaning structures and action. | Disorienting dilemma, critical reflection, rational discourse, perspective transformation. | Highlights the importance of critical reflection and perspective-taking in learning and behavior change. | Can be overly focused on individual cognitive processes; may overlook social or structural factors. | Mezirow (1991) |
| **Prospect Theory** | People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point and are disproportionately sensitive to losses versus equivalent gains, leading to systematic departures from expected-utility maximization. | Reference dependence, loss aversion, probability weighting, framing effects. | Useful for understanding and predicting decision-making behavior in uncertain or risky situations. | Primarily focused on cognitive processes; may not account for emotional or social influences on decision-making. | Kahneman & Tversky (1979) |
| **Protection Motivation Theory** | Behavioral motivation arises from threat appraisal (perceived severity and vulnerability) and coping appraisal (response efficacy and self-efficacy), determining whether a protective action is adopted. | Perceived severity, perceived vulnerability, response efficacy, self-efficacy, maladaptive responses. | Useful for designing effective health communication and promotion strategies. | Assumes a level of rationality and cognitive processing that may not be present in all individuals or situations. | Rogers (1975) |
| **Social Norms Theory** | Human behavior is strongly guided by perceptions of what others actually do (descriptive norms) and what others approve or disapprove of (injunctive norms), and these two norm types can interact or conflict. | Descriptive norms, injunctive norms, norm salience, focus theory of normative conduct. | Highlights the importance of social influence and norms in shaping behavior; applicable in many contexts. | Can be difficult to measure or quantify; may underestimate individual cognitive processes. | Cialdini et al. (1990) |
| **Environmental Identity Theory** | Individuals incorporate their relationship with nature into their self-concept, and the strength of this environmental identity predicts environmental attitudes, concern, and behavior. | Environmental identity, self-concept, identity salience, identity-congruent behavior. | Useful for understanding and fostering pro-environmental behavior and attitudes. | Primarily focused on individual identity and values; may not address broader structural or systemic factors. | Clayton (2003) |
| **Place Attachment Theory** | People develop affective, cognitive, and behavioral bonds to specific places, and the strength and nature of these bonds predict place-protective behaviors including environmental stewardship. | Place identity, place dependence, place affect, person-place bonding, stewardship behavior. | Highlights the importance of place attachment and identity in promoting environmental stewardship and protection. | Can be difficult to measure or quantify; not all individuals or cultures may share the same conceptualization of place. | Scannell & Gifford (2010) |
| **Moral Foundations Theory** | Human moral reasoning rests on multiple innate foundations (care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity, liberty) that vary in relative emphasis across individuals and cultures, shaping divergent responses to conservation and environmental messages. | Care/harm, fairness/cheating, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, sanctity/degradation, liberty/oppression. | Useful for understanding and addressing value-based conflicts in conservation and environmental communication. | Can be overly focused on individual moral reasoning; may not address broader social or structural factors. | Haidt (2012); Graham et al. (2013) |
| **Social Practice Theory** | The primary unit of analysis for understanding behavior should be the "practice" (a routinized configuration of materials, competences, and meanings) rather than individual attitudes or choices. | Practices, materials, competences, meanings, routinization, practice-as-entity vs. practice-as-performance. | Provides a comprehensive framework for understanding and analyzing social practices and behaviors. | Can be overly focused on routines and practices; may overlook individual agency or structural factors. | Shove et al. (2012) |
| **Structuration Theory** | Social structures (rules and resources) and human agency are mutually constitutive: structures constrain and enable action, while action reproduces and transforms structures over time. | Structure, agency, duality of structure, rules, resources, social reproduction. | Provides a robust lens for understanding how institutions (like property rights) change through everyday actions. | Can be deeply theoretical and difficult to operationalize into testable survey constructs. | Giddens (1984) |
| **Cognitive Dissonance Theory** | Individuals experience psychological discomfort when holding conflicting beliefs or behaviors, motivating them to resolve the inconsistency. | Dissonance, consistency, rationalization, attitude change. | Highly useful in creating behavior change by highlighting the gap between an individual's pro-environmental values and actual behaviors. | Can sometimes lead to denial or rationalization rather than actual behavior change. | Festinger (1957); Stern (2018) |
| **Extended Parallel Process Model (EPPM / Fear Appeals)** | Fear-based messages are effective only when the audience feels they have the efficacy to avert the threat; otherwise, they react defensively. | Perceived threat (severity, susceptibility), perceived efficacy (self, response), danger control, fear control. | Provides a framework for designing effective risk and crisis communications. | Fear appeals can backfire (causing fatalism or avoidance) if efficacy isn't strongly established. | Witte (1992); Stern (2018) |
| **Motivation Crowding Theory** | External incentives (like money) can sometimes undermine or "crowd out" intrinsic motivation to perform a pro-social or pro-environmental behavior. | Intrinsic motivation, extrinsic rewards, crowding out, crowding in. | Crucial for designing conservation payment schemes (PES) and policy incentives without harming intrinsic stewardship. | Predicting exactly when and if crowding out will occur is often context-dependent and difficult. | Frey (1997); Stern (2018) |
| **Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs** | Human needs are arranged in a hierarchy, from physiological and safety needs to self-actualization. Higher needs become salient only when lower ones are satisfied. | Physiological needs, safety, love/belonging, esteem, self-actualization. | Helps explain why conservation may not be a priority for communities facing food or physical insecurity. | The strict hierarchical structure is broadly criticized; needs often overlap and cultural contexts vary. | Maslow (1943); Stern (2018) |
| **Identity Theory** | Focuses on the roles individuals play in society (e.g., "farmer", "environmentalist") and how the self is composed of multiple, often hierarchically arranged role identities. | Role identity, identity salience, commitment, role behavior. | Useful when targeting communication to specific stakeholder groups based on their prominent social roles. | Individuals possess multiple, sometimes conflicting identities, complicating behavioral prediction. | Stryker (1968); Stern (2018) |
| **Self-Affirmation Theory** | People are motivated to maintain their global self-worth and integrity. When threatened by information, they normally act defensively, but a self-affirming task can open them to the threatening message. | Self-integrity, defensive responses, self-affirmation, open-mindedness. | Can be used to reduce resistance to climate change messaging by first affirming the audience's core values. | Affirmation must be carefully tailored; if it feels manipulative, it loses its effect. | Steele (1988); Stern (2018) |
| **Social Intuitionist Model of Moral Judgment** | Moral judgments are primarily the result of rapid, automatic intuitions, with deliberate reasoning serving as a post-hoc rationalization. | Moral intuition, post-hoc reasoning, social influence. | Helps explain why factual arguments fail to sway people's morally-charged opinions on contentious environmental issues. | Focuses heavily on intuition, making it difficult to design interventions based solely on rational discourse. | Haidt (2001); Stern (2018) |
| **Cultural Cognition** | Individuals form risk perceptions that conform to the values of the cultural groups they belong to (e.g., egalitarian-communitarian vs. hierarchical-individualist). | Cultural worldviews, identity-protective cognition, cultural availability. | Highly effective for understanding polarization surrounding issues like climate change or wildlife management. | Can lead to fatalism regarding public persuasion, as it suggests deep-seated cultural divides are hard to bridge. | Kahan (2006); Stern (2018) |
| **Frame Theory (Framing)** | How an issue is presented (the "frame") influences the choices people make about how to process that information. | Emphasis framing, equivalence framing, salience, schema. | Foundational for environmental communication, messaging strategies, and policy advocacy. | Frames can be manipulated ethically or unethically; effects can be short-lived if counter-frames are present. | Goffman (1974); Stern (2018) |
| **Meyer’s Cultural Map** | A framework classifying cultures along eight behavioral scales (e.g., communicating, evaluating, leading) to navigate cross-cultural communication and management. | Low/high context, direct/indirect negative feedback, egalitarian/hierarchical. | Extremely useful for running international, multi-cultural conservation projects and building equitable partnerships. | Can risk stereotyping specific nationalities if applied rigidly without considering individual variance. | Meyer (2014); Stern (2018) |
| **Trust Theory** | Trust between stakeholders (interpersonal, organizational, institutional) is essential for cooperation and risk-taking. | Ability, benevolence, integrity, trustor, trustee. | Critical for community-based conservation, co-management, and navigating human-wildlife conflict. | Trust is multi-dimensional, hard to build, easily broken, and difficult to measure precisely. | Mayer et al. (1995); Stern (2018) |
| **Principled Negotiation** | A method of conflict resolution focusing on underlying interests rather than fixed positions, inventing options for mutual gain, and using objective criteria. | Interests vs. positions, BATNA, mutual gain, objective criteria. | Vital for resolving natural resource disputes, stakeholder conflicts, and collaborative planning. | Assumes parties are willing to come to the table and act rationally, which may not hold in deep-seated conflicts. | Fisher & Ury (1981); Stern (2018) |
| **The Reasonable Person Model** | Environments and situations that support people's informational needs (building mental models, being effective) bring out the most reasonable, cooperative human behavior. | Model building, meaningful action, being effective. | Guides the design of participatory processes and public meetings to minimize frustration and hostility. | Requires significant time and resources to design supportive environments; sometimes difficult in contentious settings. | Kaplan & Kaplan (2003); Stern (2018) |
| **The Co-orientation Model** | Focuses on the relationship between two entities (e.g., a conservation NGO and a local community) and their mutual perceptions, agreement, and understanding of an issue. | Agreement, congruency, accuracy, mutual perception. | Identifies communication gaps (e.g. when an agency incorrectly assumes a community opposes a policy) for targeted outreach. | Requires dyadic data collection (surveying both sides), which can be logistically intensive. | McLeod & Chaffee (1973); Stern (2018) |
| **Herzberg’s Motivation-Hygiene Theory** | Job satisfaction is driven by "motivators" (achievement, recognition), while dissatisfaction is caused by the absence of basic "hygiene factors" (salary, working conditions). | Motivators, hygiene factors, job satisfaction. | Can inform the management and retention of conservation staff, volunteers, or community monitors. | Has faced empirical criticism regarding whether factors strictly separate into two distinct bins across all cultures. | Herzberg (1959); Stern (2018) |
| **Team Effectiveness Theory** | Successful team outcomes depend on enabling conditions such as a compelling direction, strong structure, supportive context, and expert coaching. | Enabling conditions, team processes, shared mental models. | Useful for improving the functioning of interdisciplinary research teams and collaborative conservation task forces. | The definitions of "effectiveness" can vary; complex interdisciplinary dynamics can exceed standard models. | Hackman (1987); Stern (2018) |
| **Accountability Theory** | Individuals act differently when they expect to be evaluated by and have to justify their judgments or actions to others. | Pre-decisional accountability, justification, audience effects. | Can be used to structure institutional design, monitoring, and compliance mechanisms in resource governance. | Too much accountability or the wrong type can cause rigid, risk-averse behavior and stifle innovation. | Tetlock (1983); Stern (2018) |
| **Project Risk Theory** | Focuses on identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks that threaten project delivery and outcomes. | Risk identification, risk mitigation, probability, impact. | Helpful for structured planning of conservation interventions, anticipating failure points and adaptive management. | Often heavily technical and can sometimes overlook social or political risks if not explicitly integrated. | Stern (2018) |
