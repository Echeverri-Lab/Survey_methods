# Axiology Checklist

Before deciding on your methods, evaluate the underlying values and motivations driving this research. Documenting these helps clarify the most appropriate research paradigm and ethical approach.

## 1. Core Values and Drivers
- [ ] What is the ultimate goal of this research? (e.g., advancing theoretical ecology, solving a specific conservation conflict, empowering a local community).
- [ ] Who are the primary stakeholders or beneficiaries?
- [ ] How do the researchers' own backgrounds, biases, and values intersect with this context?

## 2. Intended Use and Outcomes
- [ ] **Empirical / Neutral:** Are you aiming to produce generalizable, "objective" estimates (e.g., what percentage of landowners support a policy)? (*Leans toward Positivist/Surveys*).
- [ ] **Interpretive / Contextual:** Are you aiming to deeply understand how a specific group makes meaning of an ecological phenomenon? (*Leans toward Constructivist/Qualitative*).
- [ ] **Pragmatic / Management-Driven:** Is the primary aim to find a functional solution to a management problem? (*Leans toward Pragmatism/Mixed Methods*).
- [ ] **Advocacy / Critical:** Is the research explicitly intended to challenge power dynamics or advocate for an marginalized group? (*Leans toward Critical/Participatory Methods*).

## 3. Ethical and Engagement Considerations
- [ ] What is the ethical impact of the chosen methodology on the community? 
- [ ] Will standardized instruments be too rigid, culturally inappropriate, or extractive?
- [ ] Do stakeholders need to be involved in co-designing the research questions or instruments?

*Keep this checklist with your study design documentation to justify your paradigm choice.*
