# Summary: Brown & Dueñas (2020) — selecting a research paradigm

This note extracts elements from Brown & Dueñas (2020) that are directly transferable to ecological and conservation research. The original article offers a clear flow from values/axiology → ontology → epistemology → methodology → methods → sources, and maps paradigms (positivist, post-positivist, constructivist, critical) to typical methods and data sources.

## Transferable lessons for ecological research

1. Start with axiology (what you value)
- Make explicit what outcomes, stakeholders, or values motivate the research (e.g., biodiversity protection, community livelihoods, animal welfare).
- Axiological clarity helps determine whether research should be activist, advocacy-oriented, or neutral/empirical; it also shapes ethical choices and participant engagement.

2. Map ontology → epistemology → methodology → methods
- For ecology, map these as:
  - Ontology: Is the ecological reality being studied viewed as objectively measurable (species counts, habitat metrics) or as socially constructed (local meanings of species, cultural values around landscapes)?
  - Epistemology: What knowledge counts? Statistical, repeatable measures or situated local knowledge?
  - Methodology: Experiments, long-term monitoring, surveys, ethnography, mixed-methods designs.
  - Methods: Specific tools (transects, sensor data, structured questionnaires, interviews, participatory GIS).

3. Choose methods that align with the question and the type of knowledge sought
- If the question asks "how many" or "how strongly" → statistical/quantitative approaches (surveys, monitoring).
- If the question asks "how" or "why" or seeks meaning → qualitative methods.
- If both are needed, design mixed-methods studies (convergent or sequential).

4. Practical decision rules from the flowchart
- If you value generalizability and have measurable constructs → prioritize surveys/experimental designs.
- If you value contextualized understanding, reflexivity, and power dynamics → prioritize qualitative and participatory approaches.
- If you can combine: use qualitative phases to develop constructs and instruments, then sample for generalization.

5. Data sources and examples adapted to ecology
- Quantitative sources: field surveys, sensor/remote-sensing data, structured questionnaires, monitoring datasets, experiments (manipulative plots).
- Qualitative sources: interviews with local actors, participant observation, ethnographic notes, local ecological knowledge (LEK), focus groups.
- Mixed/other: participatory mapping, community workshops, ethnographic-informed survey items, administrative or citizen-science records.

6. Document epistemological choices
- Brown & Dueñas stress documenting and justifying your paradigm choice; in ecology, state whether you adopt a positivist lens (e.g., to estimate population trends), interpretivist (to understand stakeholder values), or pragmatist (to solve a real-world management problem).

## Concrete, ecologically-relevant recommendations
- Add an explicit "Axiology" step to Step 01: list stakeholder values and intended use of results (management decision, theory testing, advocacy).
- Use a short checklist to decide method class (survey vs qualitative vs mixed):
  - Is a quantitative estimate of prevalence/association essential? (Survey)
  - Are meanings, governance, power, or local knowledge central? (Qualitative)
  - Will results inform management or policy that requires both representative estimates and contextual acceptability? (Mixed)
- When developing surveys in ecology, always do formative qualitative work where LEK or local categories may differ from academic constructs.
- Consider alternative data sources (remote sensing, automated sensors, administrative records) when self-report is insufficient.
