# Inferential Analysis Guide for Survey Research Data
 
This guide maps research objectives and data types to appropriate inferential analyses, including key assumptions and corresponding R packages.

Analysis methods are grouped by similarity in objective (i.e., Reliability and Validity, etc.). Data type specifies independent variable (IV) and dependent variable (DV) when applicable. R package shows the name of the package used for this analysis in R; the command used for the analysis is shown in brackets. 
 
---

## Dimensionality, Classification, and Clustering
 
| Analysis method | Objective | Data Type | Assumptions | R package |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) | Identify underlying latent constructs without a priori structure | Interval or continuous | 1. Sample size is adequate (commonly ≥ 5–10 observations per variable; total N ≥ 100–200). 2. Observed variables are interrelated but not perfectly collinear. 3. Relationships among variables are approximately linear. 4. The common factor model is appropriate (i.e., covariation is attributable to shared latent factors). | `psych` [`fa()`] |
| Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) | Test whether data fit a theoretically specified factor structure | Interval or continuous | 1. Sample size is adequate (commonly N ≥ 200; larger samples needed for complex models). 2. Observed variables follow a multivariate normal distribution, or robust estimators (e.g., MLR) are employed. 3. The model is correctly specified and identified (i.e., degrees of freedom ≥ 0). 4. Measurement errors are uncorrelated unless explicitly modeled. | `lavaan` [`cfa()`] |
| Principal Components Analysis (PCA) | Reduce dimensionality by summarizing variance into composite components | Interval or continuous | 1. Sample size is adequate (commonly ≥ 5–10 observations per variable; total N ≥ 100). 2. Relationships among variables are approximately linear. 3. Variables are measured on a continuous or interval scale. 4. No extreme outliers are present, as PCA is sensitive to influential observations. | `stats` [`prcomp()`] |
| Latent Class Analysis (LCA) | Identify unobserved subgroups within a population based on response patterns | Categorical | 1. Within each latent class, observed indicators are mutually independent (local independence assumption). 2. The number of classes is correctly specified (evaluated via fit indices: AIC, BIC, entropy). 3. Sample size is adequate to support stable estimation of the specified number of classes. 4. The population is a finite mixture of the specified latent classes. | `poLCA` [`polCA()`] |
| Cluster Analysis | Group respondents into distinct segments based on similarity in response patterns | Flexible; data type must match the chosen distance metric (e.g., Euclidean for continuous, Gower's for mixed types) | 1. Variables are appropriately scaled prior to analysis, as results are sensitive to measurement scale. 2. The chosen distance metric (e.g., Euclidean, Manhattan) and linkage method are appropriate for the data structure. 3. The number of clusters is specified or determined via validated criteria (e.g., elbow method, silhouette width). 4. Observations are independent. | `cluster` [`agnes()`, `diana()`, `pam()`] |

## Regressions, Predictions, and Group Comparisons
 
| Analysis method | Objective | Data Type | Assumptions | R package |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| (Multiple) Linear Regression | Examine the effect of one or more predictors on a continuous outcome, and quantify the proportion of outcome variance explained | **IV:** Continuous, ordinal (if treated as interval), or categorical (dummy-coded)<br>**DV:** Continuous | 1. Observations are independent. 2. The relationship between each predictor and the outcome is linear. 3. Residuals are normally distributed (particularly important in small samples). 4. Residual variance is constant across levels of the predictors (homoscedasticity). 5. Predictors are not perfectly collinear (no multicollinearity). | `stats` [`lm()`] |
| Independent Samples t-test | Compare differences between the means of two independent groups | **IV:** Categorical (2 independent groups)<br>**DV:** Continuous | 1. Observations are independent within and across groups. 2. The outcome is approximately normally distributed within each group (less critical for large samples; n ≥ 30 per group by central limit theorem). 3. Variances are equal across groups (homogeneity of variance; evaluate with Levene's test; use Welch correction if violated). | `stats` [`t.test()`] |
| Wilcoxon Mann-Whitney U Test | Compare distributions between two independent groups; non-parametric alternative to the independent samples t-test | **IV:** Categorical (2 independent groups)<br>**DV:** Continuous or ordinal | 1. Observations are independent within and across groups. 2. The outcome is at least ordinally scaled. 3. The distribution of the outcome has a similar shape across groups (required for interpreting results as a test of medians; not required if interpreted as stochastic dominance). | `stats` [`wilcox.test()`] |
| Paired Samples t-test | Compare means across two paired or repeated measurements within the same participants | **IV:** Categorical (2 repeated or matched conditions)<br>**DV:** Continuous | 1. The within-pair differences are approximately normally distributed (less critical for large samples). 2. Observations are paired (each participant or unit contributes exactly two measurements). 3. Pairs are independent of one another. | `stats` [`t.test(paired = TRUE)`] |
| Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test | Compare median difference between two paired or repeated measurements; non-parametric alternative to the paired samples t-test | **IV:** Categorical (2 repeated or matched conditions)<br>**DV:** Continuous or ordinal | 1. The outcome is at least ordinally scaled. 2. Observations are paired (each participant or unit contributes exactly two measurements). 3. The distribution of within-pair differences is symmetric around the median. 4. Pairs are independent of one another. | `stats` [`wilcox.test(paired = TRUE)`] |
| One-Way ANOVA | Test whether the means of a continuous outcome differ significantly across three or more independent groups | **IV:** Categorical (3+ independent groups)<br>**DV:** Continuous | 1. Observations are independent. 2. Residuals are approximately normally distributed within each group. 3. Variances are approximately equal across groups (homogeneity of variance; evaluate with Levene's test). | `stats` [`aov()` or `lm()`] |
| Kruskal-Wallis Test | Compare distribution of an ordinal or ranked outcome across three or more independent groups; non-parametric alternative to one-way ANOVA | **IV:** Categorical (3+ independent groups)<br>**DV:** Continuous or ordinal | 1. Observations are independent. 2. The outcome is at least ordinally scaled. 3. Distribution shapes are similar across groups (required for interpreting results as a test of medians; not required if interpreted as stochastic dominance). | `stats` [`kruskal.test()`] |
| Repeated Measures ANOVA | Compare means of a continuous outcome across 3+ paired or repeated measurements within the same participants | **IV:** Categorical (3+ repeated or matched conditions)<br>**DV:** Continuous | 1. Observations are independent across participants. 2. Residuals are approximately normally distributed. 3. The covariance matrix of the repeated measurements satisfies sphericity (evaluate with Mauchly's test; apply Greenhouse–Geisser or Huynh–Feldt correction if violated). | `ez` [`ezANOVA()`], `afex` [`aov_ez()`] |
| Friedman Test | Compare distributions of an ordinal outcome across 3+ paired or repeated measurements; non-parametric alternative to repeated measures ANOVA | **IV:** Categorical (3+ repeated or matched conditions)<br>**DV:** Continuous or ordinal | 1. Observations are paired or blocked (each participant contributes measurements at each condition). 2. The outcome is at least ordinally scaled. 3. Blocks (participants) are independent of one another. | `stats` [`friedman.test()`] |
| Logistic Regression | Predict the probability of a binary outcome from one or more predictors, and estimate the association between predictors and the log-odds of the outcome | **IV:** Continuous, ordinal (if treated as interval), or categorical (dummy-coded)<br>**DV:** Categorical (binary) | 1. Observations are independent. 2. The log-odds of the outcome is linearly related to each continuous predictor. 3. Predictors are not perfectly collinear. 4. Sample size is adequate (commonly ≥ 10–20 events per predictor). | `stats` [`glm(family = binomial)`] |
| Multinomial Logistic Regression | Predict membership in one of three or more unordered outcome categories from one or more predictors | **IV:** Continuous, ordinal (if treated as interval), or categorical (dummy-coded)<br>**DV:** Categorical (unordered, 3+ categories) | 1. Observations are independent. 2. The log-odds of each outcome category relative to the reference category is linearly related to each continuous predictor. 3. Predictors are not perfectly collinear. 4. Sample size is adequate to support stable estimation across all outcome categories. | `nnet` [`multinom()`] |
| Ordinal Logistic Regression | Predict membership in one of three or more ordered outcome categories from one or more predictors | **IV:** Continuous, ordinal (if treated as interval), or categorical (dummy-coded)<br>**DV:** Ordinal (3+ ordered categories) | 1. Observations are independent. 2. The proportional odds assumption holds: the relationship between each predictor and the log-odds of the outcome does not vary across response thresholds (evaluate with Brant test). 3. Predictors are not perfectly collinear. | `MASS` [`polr()`] |
| Mediation Analysis | Test whether a mediating variable (M) explains the mechanism linking a predictor (X) to an outcome (Y), and estimate direct, indirect, and total effects | **IV** (predictor X): Continuous or categorical<br>**DV** (outcome Y): Continuous<br>**Mediator** (M): Continuous | 1. Causal ordering is correctly specified (X → M → Y). 2. There are no unmeasured confounders of the X–M, X–Y, or M–Y relationships. 3. Observations are independent. 4. Bootstrapping (recommended: ≥ 1,000–5,000 resamples) is used to construct confidence intervals for indirect effects, as the sampling distribution of indirect effects is typically non-normal. | `mediation` [`mediate()`] |
| MANOVA | Test whether the means of two or more continuous outcomes differ simultaneously across independent groups, while accounting for intercorrelations among outcomes | **IV:** Categorical (2+ independent groups)<br>**DV:** Continuous (multivariate; 2+ outcomes) | 1. Observations are independent. 2. Outcome variables follow a multivariate normal distribution within each group. 3. Covariance matrices are approximately equal across groups (homogeneity of covariance; evaluate with Box's M test). 4. Outcome variables are correlated but not perfectly collinear. | `stats` [`manova()`] |
| PERMANOVA | Test whether multivariate response profiles differ across groups using permutation-based inference; non-parametric alternative to MANOVA | **IV:** Categorical (2+ independent groups)<br>**DV:** Multivariate; any type representable as a distance or dissimilarity matrix | 1. Observations are independent. 2. Multivariate dispersion (spread) is approximately equal across groups; heterogeneity of dispersion can inflate Type I error and should be evaluated with PERMDISP (`betadisper()`) prior to interpretation. 3. The chosen distance metric is appropriate for the data type (e.g., Bray–Curtis for compositional data, Euclidean for continuous data). | `vegan` [`adonis2()`] |

## Relational and Hierarchical Models
 
| Analysis method | Objective | Data Type | Assumptions | R package |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Multilevel Modeling (MLM) | Model outcome variables while accounting for the non-independence introduced by nested or hierarchical data structures (e.g., respondents nested within sites, schools, or regions) | **IV:** Continuous or categorical at one or more levels of the hierarchy<br>**DV:** Continuous (standard MLM); extensions available for binary, count, and ordinal outcomes | 1. Observations are independent across higher-level units (e.g., clusters), though within-cluster dependence is explicitly modeled. 2. Random effects are normally distributed at each level of the hierarchy. 3. Residuals are normally distributed at the lowest level (for Gaussian MLM). 4. Sample size is adequate at each level of the hierarchy (commonly ≥ 30–50 higher-level units for stable estimation of random effects). | `lme4` [`lmer()`, `glmer()`] |
| Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) | Reduce complex dissimilarity or distance matrices to a low-dimensional space for visualization and interpretation | Flexible; accepts any data type reducible to a valid pairwise dissimilarity matrix | 1. The input dissimilarity or distance matrix validly represents the pairwise (dis)similarities among objects. 2. The number of dimensions for the solution is specified by the researcher and evaluated using a stress plot (scree plot of stress values). 3. No distributional assumptions are imposed on the data. | `stats` [`cmdscale()`] |
| Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) | Simultaneously estimate complex networks of relationships among observed and latent variables, integrating measurement models (CFA) with structural path models | Continuous or interval; ordinal data requires robust estimators (e.g., WLSMV) | 1. Sample size is adequate (commonly N ≥ 200; larger samples are required for complex models). 2. Observed variables follow a multivariate normal distribution, or robust estimators (e.g., MLR, WLSMV) are employed. 3. The model is correctly specified and identified (degrees of freedom ≥ 0). 4. Measurement errors are uncorrelated unless explicitly modeled. | `lavaan` [`sem()`] |

## Correlation and Association
 
| Analysis method | Objective | Data Type | Assumptions | R package |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pearson Correlation | Assess the strength and direction of the linear association between two continuous variables | **IV:** Continuous<br>**DV:** Continuous | 1. The relationship between the two variables is linear. 2. Both variables are approximately normally distributed (bivariate normality; less critical for large samples). 3. No extreme outliers are present, as Pearson r is sensitive to influential observations. 4. Observations are independent. | `base R` [`cor()`], `Hmisc` [`rcorr()`] for correlation matrices |
| Spearman's Rank Correlation | Assess the strength and direction of the monotonic association between two ranked or ordinal variables; non-parametric alternative to Pearson correlation | **IV:** Ordinal or continuous<br>**DV:** Ordinal or continuous | 1. The relationship between the two variables is monotonic (not necessarily linear). 2. Both variables are at least ordinally scaled. 3. Observations are independent. 4. No extreme tied ranks (a large proportion of ties can distort the statistic). | `stats` [`cor(method = 'spearman')`] |
| Chi-Square Test of Independence | Assess whether two categorical variables are associated | **IV:** Categorical (2+ unordered categories)<br>**DV:** Categorical (2+ unordered categories) | 1. Observations are independent. 2. Expected cell frequencies are ≥ 5 in all cells (if violated, use Fisher's exact test for 2×2 tables or collapse categories). 3. Each observation is counted in exactly one cell of the contingency table. | `stats` [`chisq.test()`] |
