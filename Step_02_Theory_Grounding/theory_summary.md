# Theory Summary

This table summarizes specific theories, key frameworks, and theory syntheses regarding human action and transformative change drawn from the collected literature.

| Theory / Framework | Main Argument | Main Constructs | Source |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Behavioral Decision Theory** | Individuals make decisions based on cognitive processes that deviate from perfect rationality, relying on heuristics. | Expected utility, heuristics, cognitive biases, value. | Edwards (1954); Tversky & Kahneman (1974) |
| **Behavioral Intention Theories (TRA, TPB, TAM)** | Behavioral intention is the primary driver of behavior and is influenced by attitudes, subjective norms, usefulness, and perceived control. | Attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioral control, perceived ease of use. | Fishbein & Ajzen (1975); Davis (1989); Ajzen (1991) |
| **Cognitive Load Theory** | Learning and task execution are constrained by working memory capacity; systems should be designed to optimize this load. | Intrinsic cognitive load, extraneous cognitive load, germane cognitive load, working memory. | Sweller (1988); Sweller et al. (1998) |
| **Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM)** | Persuasion operates through two routes (central and peripheral) depending on the user's motivation and ability to process information. | Central route processing, peripheral route processing, motivation, ability to process. | Petty & Cacioppo (1986) |
| **Flow Theory** | Optimal human experience occurs when there is a perfect balance between personal skill and task challenge, causing deep immersion. | Skill level, challenge magnitude, immersion/absorption, goal clarity, immediate feedback. | Csikszentmihalyi (1975, 1990) |
| **Homo Ludens (Play Theory)** | Play is a primary, voluntary element of human culture that exists in a "magic circle" separate from ordinary life. | Magic circle, voluntary participation, rules, meaning creation, playfulness. | Huizinga (1938) |
| **Information Processing Theory** | The human mind operates similarly to a computer, processing information through sensory, working, and long-term memory. | Sensory memory, working memory, long-term memory, encoding, retrieval. | Miller (1956); Schunk (1996) |
| **Keller’s ARCS Motivational Model** | Instructional (and system) design must explicitly account for the motivation to learn by securing attention and building relevance. | Attention, Relevance, Confidence, Satisfaction (ARCS). | Keller (1987) |
| **Organizational Learning Theory** | Organizations learn and adapt over time by processing information from their environment and modifying internal behaviors/structures. | Knowledge acquisition, information distribution, interpretation, organizational memory. | Argyris & Schön (1978); Daft & Weick (1984) |
| **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** | Human motivation is driven by intrinsic psychological needs, and environments that support these needs foster the highest engagement. | Autonomy, competence, relatedness, intrinsic motivation, extrinsic motivation. | Deci & Ryan (1985, 2000) |
| **Social Cognitive Theory / Social Learning** | People learn by observing others within the context of social interactions, experiences, and outside media, mediated by their own beliefs. | Observational learning, self-efficacy, reciprocal determinism, behavioral reinforcement. | Bandura (1977, 1986) |
| **8 Metatheories of Human Action** | Human action theories across disciplines can be organized into eight fundamental assumptions (metatheories), such as "independent," "needs," "top-down," and "interdependent" metatheories. No single metatheory addresses all problems; integration is needed to tackle complex social-environmental crises. | Independent vs. Interdependent Variables, Needs/Motivations, Structural/Top-Down Constraints, Metatheory frameworks. | Eyster et al. (2022) |
| **Use of Human Action Theories in Conservation** | Most conservation research fails to leverage the full richness of human action theories. The field relies heavily on the *Theory of Planned Behavior* (driven by the "independent self" metatheory), limiting its capacity to foster transformative structural shifts in conservation. | Social Science Theory, Human Action, Theory of Planned Behavior, Independent Self Metatheory, Conservation Behavior. | Eyster et al. (2025) |
| **Gamification Theory Agenda** | Gamification in non-gaming systems lacks theoretical grounding; understanding its impacts requires integrating existing IS and psychological theories (like Self-Determination Theory, Flow, and Information Processing) into a cohesive research agenda for behavioral and attitudinal change. | Gamification, Attitudinal Change, Behavioral Change, Intrinsic/Extrinsic Motivation, Fun/Playfulness. | Treiblmaier et al. (2018) |
| **Integrative Framework for Transformative Social Change** | Achieving environmental sustainability requires overcoming the gap between reductionist (individual-focused) and holistic (structural) paradigms. Transformative change emerges from multi-directional interactions involving private, social-signaling, and system-changing actions. | Private Action, Social-Signaling Action, System-Changing Action, Social Structures, Reductionism vs. Holism. | Naito et al. (2022) |
