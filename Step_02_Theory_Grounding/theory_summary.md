# Theory Summary

This table summarizes specific theories, key frameworks, and theory syntheses regarding human action and transformative change drawn from the collected literature.

| Theory / Framework | Main Argument | Main Constructs | Source |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Behavioral Decision Theory** | Individuals make decisions based on cognitive processes that deviate from perfect rationality, relying on heuristics. | Expected utility, heuristics, cognitive biases, value. | Edwards (1954); Tversky & Kahneman (1974) |
| **Behavioral Intention Theories (TRA, TPB, TAM)** | Behavioral intention is the primary driver of behavior and is influenced by attitudes, subjective norms, usefulness, and perceived control. | Attitude, subjective norms, perceived behavioral control, perceived ease of use. | Fishbein & Ajzen (1975); Davis (1989); Ajzen (1991) |
| **Cognitive Load Theory** | Learning and task execution are constrained by working memory capacity; systems should be designed to optimize this load. | Intrinsic cognitive load, extraneous cognitive load, germane cognitive load, working memory. | Sweller (1988); Sweller et al. (1998) |
| **Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM)** | Persuasion operates through two routes (central and peripheral) depending on the user's motivation and ability to process information. | Central route processing, peripheral route processing, motivation, ability to process. | Petty & Cacioppo (1986) |
| **Flow Theory** | Optimal human experience occurs when there is a perfect balance between personal skill and task challenge, causing deep immersion. | Skill level, challenge magnitude, immersion/absorption, goal clarity, immediate feedback. | Csikszentmihalyi (1975, 1990) |
| **Homo Ludens (Play Theory)** | Play is a primary, voluntary element of human culture that exists in a "magic circle" separate from ordinary life. | Magic circle, voluntary participation, rules, meaning creation, playfulness. | Huizinga (1938) |
| **Information Processing Theory** | The human mind operates similarly to a computer, processing information through sensory, working, and long-term memory. | Sensory memory, working memory, long-term memory, encoding, retrieval. | Miller (1956); Schunk (1996) |
| **Keller’s ARCS Motivational Model** | Instructional (and system) design must explicitly account for the motivation to learn by securing attention and building relevance. | Attention, Relevance, Confidence, Satisfaction (ARCS). | Keller (1987) |
| **Organizational Learning Theory** | Organizations learn and adapt over time by processing information from their environment and modifying internal behaviors/structures. | Knowledge acquisition, information distribution, interpretation, organizational memory. | Argyris & Schön (1978); Daft & Weick (1984) |
| **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** | Human motivation is driven by intrinsic psychological needs, and environments that support these needs foster the highest engagement. | Autonomy, competence, relatedness, intrinsic motivation, extrinsic motivation. | Deci & Ryan (1985, 2000) |
| **Social Cognitive Theory / Social Learning** | People learn by observing others within the context of social interactions, experiences, and outside media, mediated by their own beliefs. | Observational learning, self-efficacy, reciprocal determinism, behavioral reinforcement. | Bandura (1977, 1986, 2001) |
| **8 Metatheories of Human Action** | Human action theories across disciplines can be organized into eight fundamental assumptions (metatheories), such as "independent," "needs," "top-down," and "interdependent" metatheories. No single metatheory addresses all problems; integration is needed to tackle complex social-environmental crises. | Independent vs. Interdependent Variables, Needs/Motivations, Structural/Top-Down Constraints, Metatheory frameworks. | Eyster et al. (2022) |
| **Narrative Transportation Theory** | Individuals become absorbed in a story context, connecting with characters and the narrative world while disconnecting from their physical environment, which results in reduced counterarguing and unique persuasive effects. | Narrative transportation, immersion, absorption, perspective-taking, counterarguing. | Green & Brock (2000) |
| **Persuasion Knowledge Model** | Consumers develop knowledge about persuasion attempts, tactics, and agents, and they use this knowledge to cope with and respond to persuasive messages. | Persuasion knowledge, agent knowledge, topic knowledge, coping mechanisms. | Friestad & Wright (1994) |
| **Associative Network Theory** | Information is stored in memory as interconnected nodes; activating one node spreads activation to related nodes, facilitating recall and processing of linked concepts. | Memory nodes, associative links, spreading activation, recall. | Collins & Loftus (1975) |
| **Cognitive Capacity Theory** | The human cognitive system has a limited pool of resources; performance and information processing depend on how these resources are allocated during tasks. | Cognitive capacity, resource allocation, processing limits. | Isen & Daubman (1984) |
| **Entertainment Overcoming Resistance Model** | Narrative structures and character involvement in entertainment media reduce various forms of audience resistance to persuasive messaging (such as psychological reactance or counterarguing). | Transportation, parasocial interaction, identification, psychological reactance. | Moyer-Gusé & Nabi (2010) |
| **Exemplification Theory** | The use of specific, emotionally engaging examples (exemplars) in media profoundly influences audience formation of risk perceptions and beliefs more strongly than base-rate statistical data. | Exemplars, base-rate information, availability heuristic, risk perception. | Zillmann (1999) |
| **Identification Theory** | An imaginative process wherein the audience member assumes the identity, goals, and perspective of a character, essentially experiencing the narrative from the inside. | Character identification, empathy, perspective-taking, absorption. | Cohen (2018) |
| **Psychological Reactance Theory** | When individuals feel their freedom of choice or action is threatened, they experience a reactionary motivational state directed toward restoring that freedom, often by rejecting a persuasive message. | Threat to freedom, psychological reactance, freedom restoration, boomerang effect. | Brehm (1966) |
| **Social Identity Theory** | An individual's self-concept and self-esteem are significantly influenced by their meaningful memberships in various social groups, leading to in-group favoritism. | Social categorization, social identification, social comparison, in-group bias. | Hogg & Turner (1987) |
| **Taylor's Strategy Wheel** | A strategic framework in marketing communication that categorizes message strategies across transmission (informational, ego, social) and ritual (sensory, routine, acute) dimensions. | Transmission view, ritual view, informational/ego/social strategies. | Taylor (1999) |
| **Dual Processing Models** | Attitude change and persuasion occur via distinct processing routes (e.g., careful cognitive elaboration vs. superficial cues) depending on the individual's motivation and cognitive capacity. | Elaboration likelihood, central route, peripheral route, motivation, ability. | Petty et al. (1983) |
| **Use of Human Action Theories in Conservation** | Most conservation research fails to leverage the full richness of human action theories. The field relies heavily on the *Theory of Planned Behavior* (driven by the "independent self" metatheory), limiting its capacity to foster transformative structural shifts in conservation. | Social Science Theory, Human Action, Theory of Planned Behavior, Independent Self Metatheory, Conservation Behavior. | Eyster et al. (2025) |
| **Gamification Theory Agenda** | Gamification in non-gaming systems lacks theoretical grounding; understanding its impacts requires integrating existing IS and psychological theories (like Self-Determination Theory, Flow, and Information Processing) into a cohesive research agenda for behavioral and attitudinal change. | Gamification, Attitudinal Change, Behavioral Change, Intrinsic/Extrinsic Motivation, Fun/Playfulness. | Treiblmaier et al. (2018) |
| **Integrative Framework for Transformative Social Change** | Achieving environmental sustainability requires overcoming the gap between reductionist (individual-focused) and holistic (structural) paradigms. Transformative change emerges from multi-directional interactions involving private, social-signaling, and system-changing actions. | Private Action, Social-Signaling Action, System-Changing Action, Social Structures, Reductionism vs. Holism. | Naito et al. (2022) |
| **Value-Belief-Norm (VBN) Theory** | Links ecological worldviews to awareness-of-consequences beliefs to ascription-of-responsibility beliefs to personal moral norms to pro-environmental action through a causal chain. | Biospheric values, ecological worldview (NEP), awareness of consequences, ascription of responsibility, personal norms. | Stern et al. (1999); Stern (2000) |
| **Norm Activation Model (NAM)** | Altruistic and pro-social behavior is activated when individuals become aware that their actions have consequences for others and ascribe responsibility for those consequences to themselves. | Awareness of consequences, ascription of responsibility, personal norms, altruistic behavior. | Schwartz (1977) |
| **New Ecological Paradigm (NEP)** | Measures the degree to which people hold an ecological (vs. anthropocentric) worldview, serving as a foundational predictor of environmental attitudes and behavior. | Ecological worldview, limits to growth, anti-anthropocentrism, balance of nature, human exemptionalism. | Dunlap & Van Liere (1978); Dunlap et al. (2000) |
| **Nature Connectedness / Connectedness to Nature** | Individuals' emotional and cognitive sense of connection with the natural world is a stable predictor of pro-environmental attitudes and behavior beyond knowledge alone. | Nature relatedness, inclusion of nature in self, emotional affinity, cognitive connectedness. | Nisbet et al. (2009); Schultz (2002) |
| **Common-Pool Resource (CPR) Theory** | Under identifiable institutional conditions, communities can self-govern shared natural resources without privatization or top-down state regulation, contrary to the "tragedy of the commons." | Design principles, self-governance, institutional arrangements, monitoring, graduated sanctions. | Ostrom (1990) |
| **Collective Action Theory** | Rational, self-interested individuals will tend to free-ride on group efforts for public goods unless selective incentives or institutional mechanisms are present. | Free-riding, public goods, group size, selective incentives, cooperation dilemma. | Olson (1965) |
| **Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) Framework** | A general analytical framework for studying how rules-in-use, biophysical conditions, and community attributes structure human interactions and outcomes in social-ecological systems. | Action arenas, rules-in-use, biophysical conditions, community attributes, outcomes. | Ostrom (2005) |
| **Social-Ecological Systems (SES) Framework** | A diagnostic, multi-tier framework for identifying the key variables and interactions that shape outcomes in linked social and ecological systems, enabling cross-case comparison. | Resource systems, resource units, governance systems, actors/users, interactions, outcomes. | Ostrom (2009) |
| **Resilience Theory / Adaptive Cycle** | Social-ecological systems cycle through phases of growth, conservation, release, and reorganization; resilience is the capacity to absorb disturbance and reorganize while retaining essential function. | Adaptive cycle (r, K, Ω, α), resilience, adaptability, transformability. | Holling (1973); Folke et al. (2004) |
| **Panarchy Theory** | Adaptive cycles are nested across spatial and temporal scales in a hierarchy (panarchy), and cross-scale interactions (revolt and remember) explain how small, fast changes can cascade upward or how large, slow structures constrain lower levels. | Nested adaptive cycles, revolt, remember, cross-scale interactions. | Gunderson & Holling (2002) |
| **Complex Adaptive Systems (CAS)** | Socio-ecological systems exhibit emergent properties, self-organization, and non-linear dynamics arising from interactions among diverse, adaptive agents, making prediction from component behavior alone insufficient. | Emergence, self-organization, non-linearity, feedback loops, agent diversity. | Holland (1995); Levin (1998) |
| **Transtheoretical Model / Stages of Change** | Behavior change proceeds through discrete motivational stages (precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance), and interventions are most effective when matched to the individual's current stage. | Stages of change, processes of change, decisional balance, self-efficacy. | Prochaska & DiClemente (1983) |
| **COM-B Model / Behavior Change Wheel** | Behavior is a product of the interaction between Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation; systematic intervention design requires diagnosing which of these components is deficient. | Capability (physical/psychological), Opportunity (physical/social), Motivation (reflective/automatic). | Michie et al. (2011) |
| **Diffusion of Innovations** | Innovations spread through social systems via communication channels over time, with adoption following a predictable S-curve driven by adopter categories from innovators to laggards. | Innovation attributes (relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, observability), adopter categories, communication channels. | Rogers (1962, 2003) |
| **Multi-Level Perspective (MLP) on Sustainability Transitions** | Sustainability transitions result from the dynamic interplay between innovative niches, prevailing sociotechnical regimes, and exogenous landscape pressures. | Niche innovations, sociotechnical regime, sociotechnical landscape, windows of opportunity. | Geels (2002) |
| **Transformative Learning Theory** | Perspective transformation occurs when disorienting dilemmas trigger critical reflection on taken-for-granted assumptions, leading to revised meaning structures and action. | Disorienting dilemma, critical reflection, rational discourse, perspective transformation. | Mezirow (1991) |
| **Prospect Theory** | People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point and are disproportionately sensitive to losses versus equivalent gains, leading to systematic departures from expected-utility maximization. | Reference dependence, loss aversion, probability weighting, framing effects. | Kahneman & Tversky (1979) |
| **Protection Motivation Theory** | Behavioral motivation arises from threat appraisal (perceived severity and vulnerability) and coping appraisal (response efficacy and self-efficacy), determining whether a protective action is adopted. | Perceived severity, perceived vulnerability, response efficacy, self-efficacy, maladaptive responses. | Rogers (1975) |
| **Social Norms Theory** | Human behavior is strongly guided by perceptions of what others actually do (descriptive norms) and what others approve or disapprove of (injunctive norms), and these two norm types can interact or conflict. | Descriptive norms, injunctive norms, norm salience, focus theory of normative conduct. | Cialdini et al. (1990) |
| **Environmental Identity Theory** | Individuals incorporate their relationship with nature into their self-concept, and the strength of this environmental identity predicts environmental attitudes, concern, and behavior. | Environmental identity, self-concept, identity salience, identity-congruent behavior. | Clayton (2003) |
| **Place Attachment Theory** | People develop affective, cognitive, and behavioral bonds to specific places, and the strength and nature of these bonds predict place-protective behaviors including environmental stewardship. | Place identity, place dependence, place affect, person-place bonding, stewardship behavior. | Scannell & Gifford (2010) |
| **Moral Foundations Theory** | Human moral reasoning rests on multiple innate foundations (care, fairness, loyalty, authority, sanctity, liberty) that vary in relative emphasis across individuals and cultures, shaping divergent responses to conservation and environmental messages. | Care/harm, fairness/cheating, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, sanctity/degradation, liberty/oppression. | Haidt (2012); Graham et al. (2013) |
| **Social Practice Theory** | The primary unit of analysis for understanding behavior should be the "practice" (a routinized configuration of materials, competences, and meanings) rather than individual attitudes or choices. | Practices, materials, competences, meanings, routinization, practice-as-entity vs. practice-as-performance. | Shove et al. (2012) |
| **Structuration Theory** | Social structures (rules and resources) and human agency are mutually constitutive: structures constrain and enable action, while action reproduces and transforms structures over time. | Structure, agency, duality of structure, rules, resources, social reproduction. | Giddens (1984) |
